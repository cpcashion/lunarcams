The Promise

There are no cameras on the Moon. Yet.

So we built the windows first, and pointed them at the real sky.

Every window on this page is a rendering — but not a fiction. The sun stands exactly where it stands over each site this minute, partway through a daylight that lasts two weeks. Earth hangs precisely where it hangs, showing the continents actually facing the Moon right now, its city lights on the true night side, its terminator where dawn genuinely is.

The stars are the 555 brightest stars in the sky, each in its measured place. When the sun sets at Aristarchus for 354 hours, it sets here too. When Earth is new and the nearside goes dark, these windows go dark with it.

One day soon, a lander will set down a camera, and the first true pixels will arrive at three hundred and eighty thousand kilometers an ocean of static away. On that day we'll swap the paint for photons. The geometry — the sun, the Earth, the stars, the long slow days — will not need to change. It was already true.
